--- title: Tillywhandland author: Richard Dearden layout: post ---

*A Victorian Quarry preserves fossils of some of the earliest cartilaginous fishes in an Early Devonian lake.*

In the village of Aberlemno in Forfarshire and you will find a series of stones. 
These monuments, erected by the Picts in the second half of the first Millennium A.D., are carved from sandstone. 
A syncretic swirl of crosses, animals, and battles intertwine across their sandstone surface.
Five minutes down the road however you will find an altogether different monument to time. 
Along a grassy avenue, tall trees stand sentinel on mountainous spoil heaps, in the ruin of an old quarry. 
In the 19th century this was a busy industrial site: men toiled in search of the same Old Red Sandstone that the 
Picts used to fashion their monuments over a millenium earlier.
This sandstone is still visible today, cropping out amongst the spoil, the bowl now filled with water.
What concerns us though is the rock that lies just above the sandstone: alternating
dark and light laminites, etched into which are the remains of ancient organisms.

The origins of the rocks that form Tillywhandland quarry lie  in a lake system which existed here over 400 million years ago, in the Lower Devonian (specifically the Lochkovian).
This lake, Lake Forfar, formed suddenly over what was previously a high energy river system (responsible for creating the underlying sandstone).
The lake's history was then one of gradual shallowing, as Lake Forfar slowly filled up with sediment, represented by a sequence of coarsening siltstones and fine sandstones.
When Lake Forfar was at its deepest however, immediately following its creation, it left a unique bit of rock.
This section of the lake's history is represented by fine laminites, which directly overlie the sandstone.
These comprise a four part cycle of clastic silts, carbonates, organics, and green clay-shale, each understood to represent a year.
Preserved within these layers are the articulated remains of fishes, arthropods, and plants, understood to have died and floated to the deoxygenated depths of the lake, where they were preserved away from predators.
While each individual lamina is only millimetres in size, it is understood to represent a year of sedimentation, making the full extent of their deposition about 2000 years.

This provides a two thousand year window into a four hundred million year old freshwater ecosystem, preserving occupants of the shallow waters of the lake margins, and possibly washed in from surrounding rivers and the shore.
Variously sized specimens of <em>Pterygotus</em>, a large eurypterid, are known.
As are <em>Cephalaspis</em>, a likely benthic jawless osteostracan.
From the shore plant fossils are known, including <em>Parka</em>, an unusual honeycomb shaped thing possibly representing algae, and <em>Zosterophyllum</em>, a branching pla nt.
Also from the shore comes a second arthropod - the millipede <em>Archidesmus</em> - preserved curled up with legs tucked in.
Coprolites are common, and may have been left by free-swimming fishes in the lake water above.

Perhaps most noteworthily about Tillywhandland though, are the wide range of "acanthodians", or spiny sharks.
These animals are some of the earliest relatives of cartilaginous fishes, and it is rare to find them in one piece.
In Tillywhandland however there are a diverse array, and what's more they are articulated.
The most common of these at Tillywhandland is <em>Mesacanthus</em>, a small, 2-6cm, toothless fish which likely fed on microorganisms.
Almost as common is <em>Ischnacanthus</em>, a larger (7-20cm) fish with many teeth mounted on its jawbones.

Less common are <em>Climatius</em> and <em>Parexus</em>, with mouths full of shark-like tooth-whorls, armoured shoulder girdles, and many fin spines.
More rarely, other acanthodians are also found, including <em>Vernicomacanthus</em>, <em>Brachyacanthus</em>, <em>Euthacanthus</em>, and <em>Uraniacanthus</em>.
All of these animals flattened, and none preserve internal anatomy of the head.
However, they still represent one of the best-known and most diverse acanthodian faunas known beyond the Man on the Hill locality, another Lochkovian site in Canada.
