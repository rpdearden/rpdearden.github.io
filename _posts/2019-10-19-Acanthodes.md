**New job, old fish: adventures with Acanthodes**

I have got a new job, and with that have made a resolution to resurrect my blogging.
I'm at the Muséum national d'histoire naturelle (MNHN) in Paris for the next year to look at two things I'm interested in: [the early evolution of cartilaginous fishes, and pharynxes](http://www.dim-map.fr/projets-soutenus/phare/).
In particular I'll be focusing on one animal: *Acanthodes*.

*Acanthodes* is a cartilaginous fish, or chondrichthyan (a relative of sharks and rays) from the Permian.
It's usually lumped into the "acanthodians": fishes known from the Silurian-Permian which are distinctively covered in a profusion of fin spines and small scales.
In recent years it's become clear that these acanthodians are not a true group, but in fact a series of groups making up the earliest relatives of sharks and rays known from articulated fossils (i.e. whole bodies rather than bits).

However, describing most acanthodians as articulated comes at the risk of over-selling how nice their fossils usually are.
Most are fish-shaped splats of scales and spines with little internal anatomy (e.g. skulls, jaws, gill skeletons) to compare to other fishes.
This makes what is a potentially useful source of information on early cartilaginous fishes difficult to mine for information.

In *Acanthodes*, though, lies an exception.
Fossils preserved in ironstone concretions from Lebach (Saarland, Germany) preserve parts of the skull, jaws, and gill skeleton of *Acanthodes* in three dimensions.
The importance of these fossils has been acknowledged for ages: the genus *Acanthodes* was erected by Louis Agassiz in 1835 and has been studied intensively since, with examples of Lebach *Acanthodes* to be found in many European museums.
Because of its uniquely detailed preservation of an "acanthodian" internal skeleton the anatomy of *Acanthodes* has lain at the crux of arguments over the relationships and early evolution of jawed vertebrates.

![Figure 1](../../../assets/images/Acanthodes_intro/Acanthodes_moulds_fig_1.2.png)

However, the hard ironstone in which the Lebach *Acanthodes* fossils are preserved is tough to work with, and in the past the only way to really visualise the skeletal elements has been to destroy them, using acid, to create moulds.
Study of *Acanthodes* is reliant on casts of these moulds (and, more recently, digital casts) which are often slightly difficult to get one's head around.
Because of this the anatomy of the skull, jaws, and gills in *Acanthodes* have been interpreted in a profusion of different ways.

What we're hoping to do this year is to get around these problems by using CT scanning to image *Acanthodes*-bearing Lebach concretions in the MNHN collections.
We are targeting specimens that were never acid prepared, and so which still contain fossilised skeletal material.
Modelling out this material will give us three-dimensional models of the skeleton of the head and pharynx in *Acanthodes*.
We hope that this will lay to rest some of the disagreement about exactly what *Acanthodes* looked like, and provide the best peek yet into the internal skeletons of these early cartilaginous fishes.

![Figure 2](../../../assets/images/Acanthodes_intro/Acanthodes_scans_fig_2.png)

We're also hoping that with this information in hand we'll be able to say something interesting about feeding strategies in early chondrichthyans.
*Acanthodes* is pretty atypical for an acanthodian - it was alive right at the end of their range in time, and went about its business as a filter feeding, eel-shaped, lake-dweller, unlike any living cartilaginous fish.
This fits into the broader early history of cartilaginous fishes where many had unusual body shapes and lifestyles no longer exhibited by their living descendants.
We hope to explore exactly what *Acanthodes* could have been doing while it was swimming around the early Permian.

That's all for now - I hope to keep on posting about dead fishes semi-regularly.
